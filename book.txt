A Note About the Voice and the Method
The first principle is that you must not
fool yourself—and you are the easiest
person to fool.
This book is written in a borrowed register. The plain talk, the impatience with fancy
words, the insistence that you show me the mechanism and not the name of the mechanism—
that manner belongs to Richard Feynman, and everybody who has read him knows it on
sight [75, 76].
So let us be honest right at the start, the way he would have insisted. These are not
his words. He never saw a lakehouse and never heard of a foundation model, and he is not
available for comment. Every sentence here was written by the two authors on the cover,
one human and one machine, and every opinion is ours. Where we lean on something he
actually said or wrote—the cargo cult lecture [75], the appendix he attached to the Challenger
report over the objections of everyone around him [76], the diagrams with the backward
legs [74]—we cite it, so you can go check, which is the only kind of respect worth paying to a
man who spent his life telling people to go check.
Why borrow the voice at all? Because the argument in this book is already his argument,
and it kept coming out in his cadence no matter how we wrote it. The book says: an answer
that cannot tell you how it knows is not an answer, it is a decoration. It says: the fluent
machine that gives you a number for every question, including the questions it has no business
answering, is running a cargo cult, and the planes are not going to land. It says: when your
data cannot settle the matter, the honest move is not to guess prettily but to go bother the
world and find out. And it says the deepest thing he ever taught anybody, which is that the
first principle is that you must not fool yourself, and you are the easiest person to fool.
There is also a technical reason, and it is not decoration either. The whole point of this
book is that a question has two halves—one running forward from the data to the answer,
one running backward from the answer to what would justify it—and that the second half
is the one everybody threw away. Now, who taught physics that the backward line is not
a mistake but the other half of the object? Who drew the positron as an electron going
the wrong way in time and made it work [74, 77]? We did not pick this voice because it is
charming. We picked it because the man understood, better than anyone, that when a thing
runs backward you should not throw it away. You should ask what it is telling you.
How this book started. This book started because somebody asked a simple question and
got a wrong answer, and the person who gave the wrong answer was me. We had just finished
another book—a long one, about how a company ought to keep track of what it actually
knows, as opposed to what it has merely noticed [78]. The last chapter of that book was
about where the idea came from: a dissertation written in 1992 about turning logic programs
into functional programs [33]. Nice chapter. Historical. The kind of thing you put at the end
so the reader can see the family tree.
v
vi
Then the other author read the finished book, all the way through, and asked the question
every practical person asks: which chapters are the solutions? Which chapters tell me what
to do? And I gave him a list. A good list! And the historical chapter, the one about 1992,
was not on it. Because it was history, you see. It was background.
He wrote back and said, in effect: you have missed the point of your own chapter. That
thing is not background. That thing is the next engine.
Now, this is the part I want you to notice, because it is the whole reason for the book
you are holding. He was right, and I was wrong, and I was wrong in a very specific and very
common way. I had filed the idea under a label—“history,” “lineage,” “theory”—and once a
thing has a label you stop looking at it. You know its name. You feel like you understand it.
This is the oldest trap there is, and the fact that I walked into it while writing a book whose
entire subject is the difference between naming a thing and knowing a thing—well. You can
laugh. I did.
The thing in the chapter, in one page. Every database in the world speaks sql, and
sql runs one way. You give it data, it gives you an answer. Data in, answer out. It is a
magnificent machine and it has one direction, like a turnstile.
But look at the questions people actually ask. Why did the northeast churn go up? What
would it take to know whether the discount is doing anything? Which customer is missing
from this list who ought to be on it? Every one of those questions runs the other way—from
the answer back toward the data, from the conclusion back toward the evidence, from the
goal back toward the experiment that would settle it. And the turnstile does not turn that
way. It cannot. There is no grammar for it.
Now, logic programming solved this in 1972. In Prolog you write down a relation once
and you can ask it in any direction you like [10]. Beautiful. And it was too expensive to
run—all that unification, all those logical variables, all that searching [17]—and at the scale
of a modern data warehouse it is not slow, it is ridiculous. So the database people gave up
the directions and kept the scale, and we have been living with the turnstile ever since.
Here is the thing in the chapter. The price was never the price of running a question
backward. The price was the price of deciding which way it runs while it is running. If
you know the direction ahead of time—and you always do! the constants are sitting right
there in the query!—then the whole expensive machine evaporates. Unification collapses into
pattern matching. The logical variables disappear. And the two directions survive as a pair
of ordinary equations: one carrying values up from the data, one carrying demand down from
the question. Two currents in one wire.
That is the 1992 result [33], and thirty-four years later it turns out to be the specification
of a query engine for a half-trillion-row lakehouse, which is a thing that did not exist when it
was written. Nobody planned that. That is just how it worked out, and I think it is worth a
book.
What kind of book this is. Not a textbook. I hate textbooks. A textbook tells you the
answer and hides the trouble, and then you have no idea why anybody bothered. This one
has three parts, and they go in the order the trouble actually goes. Part One is the trouble:
what the one-way language cannot ask, what Prolog promised, and exactly what it cost, in
numbers, so you can see for yourself why everyone quit. Part Two is the trick: modes, the
lrec construction, demand as a thing that flows downhill, and the funny business where

machine: what you build, how it runs, what each question costs, and the mode nobody has

Every reference in here is real and you can go look it up. That is not a boast, that is the

funny to write it any other way.

Chapter 1
The Language That Only Goes One

The form is never the thing.
1.1 A turnstile
I want to start with something so ordinary that nobody looks at it anymore, which is usually
where the interesting stuff is hiding.
In 1970 a man named Codd wrote a paper [1] and it changed everything. Before Codd,
if you wanted data out of a machine you had to tell the machine how to go get it—follow
this pointer, walk down that chain. Codd said: forget that. Just say what you want. A
table is a relation, a query is an operation on relations, and the machine can figure out the
how by itself. Then they built a language for it [2], and an optimizer so the machine really
could figure out the how [3], and the whole thing worked so well that fifty years later every
warehouse and every dashboard and every chatbot with a database behind it is still speaking
that language.
Terrific. Now look at it.
It runs one way. You put tables in the FROM clause and an answer comes out the bottom.
The tables are always the input. The answer is always the output. And there is no place—not
a keyword, not a clause, not a corner of the grammar—where you can hold the answer still
and ask the machine to run toward the data. It is a turnstile. It turns one way, and it turns
beautifully, and that is all it does.
Now, when I say a thing like that, the natural reaction is: so what? Of course it computes
answers from data. What else would it do? I will tell you what else. Watch.
1.2 The three things that fall out of the geometry
Look at Figure 1.1 and follow the arrows. Everything points the same way. And three things
follow from that—not from any design decision anybody made, just from the shape.
1. The answer cannot be interrogated. The result comes out and it remembers nothing.
Ask the machine “why is this row here?” and there is no such question. It threw the
derivation away at every single operator, because why would it keep it? Nobody asked for
it.

Now, the database community knows about this, and being clever people they built the
derivation-tracking back on afterward—provenance, they call it, and there is beautiful
work in it [4, 5, 6]. But stop and look at what that means. They had to bolt the backward
direction onto the outside of the language, because the language throws it away on the
inside. When people keep rebuilding a thing on the outside of your system, that thing
belongs on the inside, and you have a design problem.
2. You cannot state a goal. There is no way to hand the machine a conclusion and say:
find me the facts that would make this true. sql can check whether a row satisfies a
condition. It cannot be handed a wish and asked to search for what would grant it.
That sounds like philosophy. It is not. Every planning question on earth has exactly that
shape. What price clears this inventory? What change moves this number? What record
is missing whose presence would complete the pattern? Those are goals, and the turnstile
does not take goals.
3. Empty means nothing. Run a query, get zero rows. What did you learn? You learned
that the machine found nothing. You did not learn whether the thing is false, or whether
nobody ever looked. And the convention that papers over this—“if I can’t derive it, it’s
false” [13]—is a perfectly good rule for airline seats and an absolute disaster for knowledge,
where the difference between we checked and it’s not true and nobody has ever checked is
the entire ballgame [78].
1.3 The questions on the other side
All right. Now let us look at the questions people are actually asking these machines. Not
the questions in the textbook. The ones in the building.
• “Why did churn spike in the northeast?” That is a request for a derivation. The person
wants the chain: this, because that, because that. What can the turnstile offer? A
GROUP BY. It will slice the churn by region and hand back a table, and the person will
read the word because into it, because they are human and the table is sitting right
there next to the question. That is not an answer. That is a costume.
• “What evidence would settle whether the discount drives renewal?” That runs from a
goal backward to the evidence. There is no sql sentence that touches any part of it.

• “Which suppliers should exist in this network but don’t?” Now the answer is a row that
does not exist. The logicians have a name for this—abduction [38]—and the turnstile’s
inputs are, by construction, only the things that are.
And the plain everyday one: the same relationship gets asked in four directions in one
week—which customers did this rep close, which rep closed this customer, which pairs look
funny—and you write four queries, and maintain four dashboards, and the single underlying
fact that they are one relation wearing four hats is expressed nowhere in the system. It lives
in somebody’s head, and then that person leaves.
1.4 The interface, and why the trouble is now
For fifty years there was a human buffer. You asked the analyst the backward question, and
the analyst—being a person, with judgment—translated it into forward queries, ran them,
thought about it, and told you what they thought. The losses in that translation were real
but they were absorbed by a salary.
The buffer is gone. Now you type the backward question into a box, in English, and
something answers you. Immediately. Fluently.
And what is it doing? It cannot run the question you asked, because no engine underneath
it can run the question you asked. So it does the only thing available: it rewrites your
question into the nearest forward one, runs that, and then narrates the result back to you in
the grammar of the question you actually asked. It says because. It says would. It says if you
had.
The machinery for this got very good very fast [48, 47]: prediction over tables, at enormous
scale, for free. Which means the paraphrase gets faster, smoother, and more confident with
every release, while the question you asked remains exactly as unanswered as Codd left it in
1970.
I want to be careful here, because I am not saying anybody is lying. Nobody is lying.
The models are doing precisely what they were built to do, and doing it magnificently. That
is the trouble. It is what Feynman called cargo cult science [75], and the thing people always
miss about that story is that the islanders were not frauds. They built the runway carefully.
They wore the headphones. They did everything right, in form. The planes did not land,
because the form was not the thing. The form is never the thing.
So: an answer that arrives in the shape of a because, with no mechanism behind it, is
a bamboo control tower. It looks like the thing. And now we are wiring it to autonomous
agents that go out and act on it, which is the point in the story where somebody gets hurt.
1.5 Fifty years of people building the missing half by
hand
Here is the tell, and once you see it you cannot unsee it. Look at what the industry has built
around the language, over and over, independently, for decades:
• Provenance systems, to recover the derivation the operators discard [4, 6].

1.6. The same hole, from inside the house
• Recursive queries, and then magic rewritings to stop the recursion from computing
the whole universe just to answer about one person—and that is Chapter 7, and it is
the funniest chapter in the book [22, 16].
• Lineage graphs in the governance layer, keeping exactly the derivation record the
query language threw away [42].
• Reverse query processing, to generate data from desired answers.
• The view-update problem, which we will get to in a moment, and which is the same
hole viewed from inside the house.
Every one of these is competent. Not one of them is a language. And their union is a
scattered, per-system, hand-built reconstruction of one single missing facility: the question,
runnable in every direction it has.
1.6 The same hole, from inside the house
One more piece of evidence, and I like this one best, because it shows the trouble arising
with nobody even asking a backward question.
A view is just a query with a name. Now: can you update through it? The user changes
the derived table and expects the base tables to change accordingly. To do that you must
run the definition backward—from the output you want, to the inputs that would produce it.
They saw this immediately, in the model’s adolescence [53]. And it turns out most views
admit many translations of an update, or none, and choosing requires information the forward
query threw away. So the relational systems did the only thing they could: they restricted
the problem to the tiny class where the backward direction happens to be unambiguous, and
everybody has lived with that ever since.
Meanwhile the programming-language people picked the problem up and made something
lovely out of it—lenses, they call them [54]: a get that runs forward, a put that runs backward,
defined together, obeying laws together. Paired. Co-defined.
Hear the rhyme? Hold onto it. In Chapter 5 the same shape is going to come back and it
is going to be the whole answer.
And notice what we have learned: the industry’s forty-year inability to update through
an interesting view, and its inability to tell you why an answer is what it is, are the same
missing current. One hole. Two symptoms. Which is very good news, actually—because it
means you only have to fix one thing.
Now. Somebody did fix it. In 1972. And then it cost too much, and everybody quit.
That is the next two chapters: the promise, and the bill.
Chapter 2
The Language That Went Every Way
Algorithm = logic + control.
—Kowalski, 1979
2.1 Write it once, ask it four ways
In 1972, in Marseille, some people built a language out of logic [10]. The raw material was
Robinson’s resolution principle [7]—a single mechanical rule for making deductions—plus
Kowalski’s insight that a certain fragment of logic, the Horn clauses, could be read as a
programming language [8]. They called the thing Prolog, and I want to show you what it
bought, because it bought exactly the thing Chapter 1 said was missing, and it bought it in
one move.
You write this:
ancestor(X, Y) :- parent(X, Y).
ancestor(X, Y) :- parent(X, Z), ancestor(Z, Y).
Two lines. An ancestor is a parent, or a parent of an ancestor. Notice what the two lines
do not say: they do not say which of X and Y you are going to know when you show up
with a question. They are silent about direction. And because they are silent about it, you
get every direction (Figure 2.1). Hand it X and it finds the descendants. Hand it Y and it
finds the ancestors. Hand it both and it says yes or no. Hand it neither and it enumerates
the whole relation, pair by pair.
Remember the fellow in Chapter 1 maintaining four dashboards for one relationship, with
the unity of the thing living only in his head? Here the unity is in the program. The four
questions are one object, and the direction you ask it from is just a property of your visit,
not of the thing itself.
There is a word for the direction, and I need you to learn it because we will use it on
every page from here on: the mode. Which argument positions arrive bound, which arrive
free. (+,-), (-,+), (+,+), (-,-). Four modes of one predicate. The mode is the formal name
for a question’s direction, and the discovery that it deserves a name at all—that “direction
of the question” is a thing, with structure, that you can analyze—is worth more than the
language it came from.
And the meaning of a program was nailed down with real rigor—van Emden and Kowalski
showed the logical reading and the computational reading agree [11]—so this was not a
gadget, it was mathematics. Kowalski put the whole philosophy on a bumper sticker:

winning x out of the bindings. The derivation is not overhead. The derivation is the product.

2.5 The fine print
Because of the fine print, which is Chapter 3, and I will not spoil the arithmetic except to
state the shape of it.
The promise says: you do not have to name the direction of your question; the machine
will discover it while running. And the machine’s mechanism for discovering it—unification,
the logical variable, backtracking search—is a marvel, and it is not free, and the price is paid
per operation, at runtime, forever, whether or not the direction was obvious the whole time.
And here is the joke that the next chapter unpacks: the direction almost always was
obvious the whole time. Your query has the constants sitting right in it. The question knew
which way it was running before the engine ever started guessing.
Kowalski’s slogan said logic and control are separate. The promise delivered the logic. The
control—when do you decide the direction?—stayed stapled to runtime, and at warehouse
scale that staple costs you somewhere between one and three orders of magnitude, which is
the difference between a product and a story.

3.1 The mechanism under the trick
The last chapter made a promise sound wonderful, and now we are going to do the thing
you must always do to a wonderful promise: open the box and price the parts. Not sneer at
it—price it. There is a difference, and the difference is a number.
The machinery under Prolog’s every-direction trick is unification [7]. Two terms unify
when there is a way of filling in their variables that makes them identical, and the algorithm
finds the most general way. That single operation is what lets a question run in any direction:
the same clause head matches whether you arrive knowing X, knowing Y, knowing both, or
knowing neither, because the matching does not care which side of the equation the unknowns
live on. It is a genuinely beautiful piece of mathematics.
Now watch what it costs. To unify, you walk two term structures, pointer by pointer.
When you hit a variable you bind it, and because the search might later fail and back up,
you must write every binding down on a trail so you can undo it. Backing up means choice
points—saved machine states, stacked up, waiting. And all the terms live on a heap of tagged
pointers, aliased every which way. Warren organized all of this into an instruction set of
real genius—the wam [17, 18], and I mean it, a masterpiece—and a masterpiece of exactly
what? Of deciding the direction of a question while the question is running. Every cell of that
machine—trail, choice points, tagged heap—is the cost of not knowing, until the moment of
the call, which way the data flows.
3.2 The arithmetic, so nobody can wave their hands
People say “Prolog is slow on big data” and they say it with a shrug, as if it were a constant
factor, a matter of tuning. It is not a constant factor, and I want to do the arithmetic in the
open so you can check me.

of 109 values per second per core. It gets that by making its unit of work a compressed
column stripe: one instruction burst decodes, tests, and projects thousands of rows at once,
in cache, in SIMD lanes, with the prefetcher humming along a predictable stride.

3.3 The retreat: keep the clauses, lose the modes
What the database people did in the 1980s was a strategic retreat, and as retreats go it was
executed brilliantly. Keep the clauses, surrender the term structure: that is Datalog—logic
programming with no function symbols, just constants and variables [15, 16]. The surrender
buys three treasures. Everything terminates, because the universe of derivable facts is
finite [11]. Everything runs set-at-a-time, because each derivation round is just relational
algebra—joins! which is the one thing the database machine is magnificent at. And it
optimizes: semi-naïve evaluation confines each round to the newly derived delta [15], which
is a seed we will harvest properly in Chapter 10.
But look carefully at the casualty report, because there is one line in it that everybody
reads past. Datalog kept the clauses and lost the modes. Bottom-up evaluation computes
the entire least fixpoint—every fact derivable from every rule—and then, at the very end,
filters for what you asked. Ask it ancestor(X,mary) over an enterprise-scale parent table
and the pure bottom-up engine will cheerfully compute everybody’s ancestors, the complete
transitive closure of the human race as recorded in your warehouse, and then keep the rows
mentioning Mary.
Do you see what happened? The retreat traded away precisely the thing the promise was
for. The direction of the question—Mary! sitting right there in the query!—no longer steers

3.4 The folklore, and the springs under the floor
Meanwhile the Prolog people, who had kept their modes, knew things they did not write
into the machine. Every working Prolog programmer knows that a predicate has directions
it is meant to run—the documentation says so, informally, in comments. The knowledge was
folklore. The machine paid the runtime price anyway, every call, because folklore is not a
compiler input.
And repairs kept springing up, each one a partial confession. Tabling—memoize subgoals,
so the engine stops re-deriving what it already derived [57, 19]—which, if you squint, is
demand-driven bottom-up evaluation: exactly the compromise a magic-set rewriting reaches
from the other side. Constraint logic programming swapped unification for domain solving [21],
a magnificent line that runs to this day. And Mercury—ah, Mercury. The Mercury people
said: what if the modes were not folklore? What if you declared them, and the compiler
generated a separate, fast, deterministic procedure per mode [20]? And it worked, and it
was fast, and you should remember Mercury when we get to Part Two, because it proves
the thesis at program scale a few years after the 1992 construction stated it at, so to speak,
equation scale.
3.5 The dilemma, on the record
So here is where the field stood, and I want it stated plainly enough that you could put it on
an index card.
You could have the directions, by deciding them at runtime, and the deciding
costs you three to five orders of magnitude against the columnar substrate—a
regime, not a factor.
Or you could have the scale, by evaluating bottom-up, set-at-a-time, and then
the question’s direction no longer steers anything, and you compute the ancestors
of the human race to learn about Mary.
Directions without scale, or scale without directions. Everybody picked scale, and
who can blame them; scale is where the money is.
But an index card has two sides, and on the back of this one, in small print, is the
assumption nobody examined: that the deciding has to happen at runtime. It does not.
The constants are in the query. The direction is known before the engine starts. The
whole dilemma rests on paying, at every single call, for a freedom—direction unknown until
now!—that the question never actually exercises.
In 1992 somebody removed that assumption [33], and the dilemma does not survive its
removal. That is Part Two, and we are done with the trouble, and now I get to show you the
trick.

4.1 The observation
Part One ended with a dilemma on an index card: directions or scale, pick one. And I said
there was fine print on the back—an assumption nobody checked. Here it is, and I am going
to give it the room it deserves, because the entire rest of the book comes out of this one
observation, and it is the kind of observation that takes thirty seconds to state and thirty-four
years to exhaust.
A logic program is direction-free. Fine. But a query never is.
Look at one. ancestor(john,Y ). By the time that question reaches any engine anywhere,
its direction is sitting in its syntax: first argument bound—there’s john, a constant, you can
see him—second argument sought. No runtime discovery is involved in knowing this. You
know it. I know it. The parser knows it.
And what does the classical machine do with this knowledge? It throws it away. The
wam re-discovers, at every single unification, at every call, millions of times a second, what
was visible in the query’s shape before the engine started—and it pays the trail, the choice
points, the tagged heap, the whole apparatus of Chapter 3, for the privilege of postponing
what it already knew [17].
So stop postponing. That is the whole trick. Make the mode part of the compilation
problem. Because look what happens when you do: a predicate under a fixed mode is not a
search problem anymore. It is a function—from its bound positions to sets of values for its
sought ones. And functions are the one thing compilers, and columnar engines, and for that
matter fifty years of computer science, know exactly how to run fast.
Definition 4.1 (Mode, moded predicate, mode family). A mode for an n-ary predicate p
is an assignment m ∈ {+,−}n of each argument position to given or sought. The moded
predicate pm is the function from bindings of the + positions to the set of tuples over the −
positions consistent with p’s definition. A predicate’s mode family is the collection {pm}
over the modes its uses actually require; the logic is written once, and the family is derived
from it.

4.2. Two witnesses that this works
Notice what the definition does to Kowalski’s slogan. Algorithm = logic + control [9] was
a beautiful separation with one loose end: it never said when control gets decided, and by
default it got decided at runtime, per call, forever. The definition moves it to compile time,
per mode, once. Same logic. Same directions. Different bill.
Now—is the query’s mode really always sitting there in the syntax? Honest answer:
nearly always, and when it is not, you can find it. The constants in a query bind positions
directly. From there, groundness propagates: if a subgoal’s inputs are bound by the time
control reaches it, its outputs are bound after it succeeds, and you can push this left to right
through a clause body like water finding its level. This is a standard abstract-interpretation
exercise [20, 58, 59], and Chapter 10 runs it on real queries. The rare genuinely-dynamic
case—direction unknown until runtime—gets a dispatch on the mode family, which costs one
branch. One branch! Against the wam’s per-unification toll. That is the exchange rate.
4.2 Two witnesses that this works
Whenever somebody shows you a trick this simple you should ask: if it’s so good, who else
uses it? Fair question. Two witnesses, both under oath.
Witness one: every database you have ever used. What does a query optimizer
do with a join? [3] It picks a direction. An access path—probe this index with these bound
keys, scan that side, build the hash on the smaller input—is a mode decision. The optimizer
takes a declarative, direction-free statement and, before execution, commits to which values
flow where. The industry has been compiling modes since 1979. It just never had the word
for what it was doing, and—this is the limitation that matters—it only ever does it for one
direction at a time, per query, with no idea that the four queries you wrote this week are one
relation. The mode family is the missing organizing object.
Witness two: Mercury. In the mid-nineties a group in Melbourne built a logic language
where modes are declared, and the compiler generates a separate specialized procedure per
mode [20]—each one deterministic where it can be, each one free of the logical-variable
machinery where the mode allows, which is nearly everywhere. Result: logic programs at
the speed of functional ones. Mercury is the existence proof at the scale of a programming
language.
The 1992 construction [33], which Chapter 5 unpacks, is the same thesis at the scale of
the underlying equations—and it came first, and it is the version that tells you what the
compiled thing is made of, which is what you need if the target is not a language runtime
but a lakehouse.
4.3 Determinism rides along
One more piece, and it comes free, which should make you suspicious, so let me show you
why it is legitimate.
Fix a mode and suddenly you can ask a question that direction-free logic cannot even
phrase: for given inputs, how many outputs? Zero-or-one? Exactly one? Zero-or-more? This
is the determinism of the moded predicate, and Mercury showed it is inferable right alongside

4.4 An old friend with a new haircut
If you have spent any time around compilers, something has been itching at you for two

scratch it properly, because knowing a trick’s family tells you what it can lift.
Specializing a general program to a known part of its input is one of the oldest ideas in
the field, old enough to have theory named after it: the Futamura projections [24], which
observe that specializing an interpreter to a program yields a compiled program—compilation
is specialization. Now say ours in that vocabulary: specializing a direction-free predicate to a
known mode yields a directed function. The mode is static input. The unification machinery
is interpretive overhead that the specialization removes. The moded predicate is the residual
program [25].
This matters for confidence. We are not inventing an exotic optimization and hoping. We
are applying the most battle-tested transformation in the compiler literature to an input—the
mode—that the field simply never noticed was static. The surprise is not the machinery. The
surprise is where the static input was hiding: in plain sight, in the question’s own shape.

Take the paired equations and apply the CPS transform—the standard translation that

inherited current inward.
And now pay attention to the order in which that happened, because the order is the
deepest lesson in the dissertation. The continuations were derived, not premised. Nobody
started from a continuation-passing runtime and tried to tame it. The starting point was
the bidirectional equations—the primary semantic object—and the continuations appeared
as their CPS image, inheriting the equations’ totality and their compiled, pattern-matching
character, the way a shadow inherits the shape of the thing that casts it.

the compilation-theoretic one, and—the point of this entire volume—the only member born
already speaking the language of queries over relations.

7.3 On the name, briefly, because it matters
I promised we would come back to the name, and I am not doing it to score a point off

magic, and everyone quotes it as if it were about the technology. It is about the observer.
The magic-set rewriting is indistinguishable from magic precisely when you do not have
the semantics in which it is a corollary. Hand a magician’s trick to the fellow who knows
the mechanism and the magic evaporates and what remains is better than magic: it is
understanding, which generalizes, which the trick never does.

Part Three, at last, is where we build the thing. And the first component is not the engine—it

upstream table restated, a model deprecated, a regime change in the market. When a warrant
dies, the ledger knows exactly which conclusions were standing on it. That is not a luxury.
That is the difference between a knowledge base and a rumor mill with indexes.

10.2 Heads become columns, matching becomes vectors

10.4 The new join generation
One development from the Datalog revival deserves its own station, because it upgrades
the promise from the constant to the exponent.
Classical planners execute a multiway join as a tree of pairwise joins, and for the cyclic

deliver a needle. The worst-case optimal join theorems [67] closed that gap: evaluate the
join attribute-at-a-time and the running time is bounded by the answer’s worst-case size, full
stop. Production engines made it real [68].
For this engine the fit is doubly happy. Part Three’s workloads—warrant traversals, skele
ton recursions, frontier assembly—are exactly the cyclic regime where worst-case optimality
pays. And the inherited current composes with it instead of competing: demand prunes the
relations first, the WCOJ kernel joins the pruned residue with guarantees, and together they
let me state the book’s slogan with the theory behind both halves: the directed question costs
its own size—in the constant, by pruning, and now in the exponent, by the join theorem.

11.1 Queries that terminate in the world
We have arrived at the mode I have been promising since the preface, and I want to introduce
it with the plainest sentence I can build:
Sometimes the correct answer to a question is an experiment.
Everybody knows this. Every scientist lives it. You ask whether the discount drives
renewal; the data in hand cannot settle it; and the honest next move is not a cleverer scan
of the same data—it is to go do something: run the holdout, mine the archive you never
opened, call the customers. The question’s last mile runs through the world.
And no query language has ever been able to say so. Ask sql a question the data cannot
settle and it hands you an empty set, indistinguishable from “false.” Ask the season’s fluent
models and they hand you a confident number, indistinguishable from knowledge. One shrugs,
the other bluffs. What you want is a machine that says: I cannot derive this; here is precisely
what is missing; here is what each way of getting it would cost; this one first. That machine
is the abductive mode, compiled, and every part we need is already on the bench.
11.2 From inference to syntax
Abduction—inference to the missing premise—has a respectable logic-programming litera
ture [38]: given a goal that fails, find the set of assumable facts which, if added, would let it
succeed. Classically this ran on the same runtime machinery as everything else in Prolog,
and inherited all of Chapter 3’s bills, plus new ones.
Watch how the compiled setting tames it. The inherited current descends, as always,
lighting the demanded region. Where the region is populated with countersigned claims,
evaluation proceeds—this is just Chapter 9’s forward machinery. Where the demand arrives
at unsigned—the fourth answer of Chapter 8—the classical engine would fail and backtrack.
The abductive compilation does something better: it catches the unmet demand and records
it. The demand current, remember, is data (Chapter 6). Unmet demand is therefore also

data: a set of typed open goals, each one saying exactly which claim, at which scope, the

11.3 Pricing it
A frontier with three open goals poses a decision problem: which to close first? And here the
mode-family cost model of Chapter 10 grows its new column. Each open goal has channels
by which it might be closed—mine the data you already own but never read; commission an
experiment; buy the dataset; transport a finding from an adjacent scope, if the transport
is licensed. Each channel has a cost, a latency, and a probability of actually resolving the
goal. And each resolution has a value: the decision downstream that goes differently once
the answer exists.
Order the channels by expected value of information per dollar, and the frontier stops
being a list and becomes a plan.
I want to give the number its provenance, because it is older than every system in this
book and it has been waiting seventy years for this plumbing. Lindley, 1956: the information
provided by an experiment is the expected reduction in uncertainty about the quantity
of interest [69]. The decision theorists converted it to currency—the expected value of
information, the difference between deciding with and without the answer [45, 46]. Bayesian
experimental design, the whole field, presupposed two things enterprises never had: a declared
space of candidate experiments, and quantified priors. Look at what the catalog holds.
The open goals are the declared space. The countersigned intervals are the priors. The
seventy-year-old number was never missing its theory; it was missing its address book—the
place where an institution’s actual questions live. The originality here is the plumbing, and I
am content with that, because plumbing is what makes theories true in buildings.

A.1 Modes and adornment
A mode for an n-ary relation is m ∈ {+,−}n (Definition 4.1); an authority-indexed mode
adds the minimum epistemic state a ∈ {exploratory,countersigned,countersigned-current}
(Chapter 9). Adornment propagates groundness through rule bodies left to right, or by
any admissible reordering (Chapter 4): a body atom’s derived mode binds every position
reachable from bound head positions and prior body bindings. Declared modes are checked;
undeclared ones are inferred by abstract interpretation over the call corpus [59, 58] and
surfaced for adoption by traffic rank.
A.2 The compiled currents
Per relation and mode, the lrec solution (Theorem 5.1) yields the inherited family (demand:
seeded by the call’s bound values; one equation per recursive site; lowered as semi-joins,
pruning predicates, and constructor matches) and the synthesized family (answers: one
equation per clause; lowered as filters, projections, joins, and unions). Evaluation disciplines,
chosen per query shape: lazy interleaving [27]; semi-naïve fixpoint [15]; worst-case-optimal
kernels for cyclic patterns [67]; differential maintenance for standing questions [39]. Fusion
across levels and currents per [73]; staging per question shape per [25].
A.3 Answer values and their duties
Every lookup returns one of four values, each with a mandatory payload: established
(interval, scope, proof-term handle); refuted (the disproof’s handle; hard-blocks licensing);
stale (the staling ledger entry; queue position; dims consequences, blocks licenses); open
(docket entry; decision-value weight). The closed-world rule [13] is admitted only per stratum,
by declaration, where closure was actually purchased; negation-as-failure [14] is licensed only
inside such strata. Anywhere else, “absent” means open, and saying otherwise is how you
fool yourself.

A.4 The four modes, operationally
Forward: consequence under scope intersection (narrowest scope on the derivation) and
state weakening (weakest state on any path). Backward: total over the established stratum;
demand refinements select warrant aspects (designs, magnitudes, currency, siblings); at the
boundary, returns the open goal. Counterfactual: gated by the identification license at the

stratum), and establishability (every goal carries an admissible channel or is returned as

A.5 Cost classes of a mode
Per mode, the planner assigns one of: materialized (stored stratum); indexed (+ positions

store (sought positions range over absent evidence; the plan’s residual is an abductive frontier).
The mode family with its cost classes is the relation’s public performance contract—and, at
the last class, its epistemic one.
A.6 Conformance, so the word means something
Bronze: moded declarations with per-mode compilation on non-recursive relations; four

standing. Gold: Silver, plus the gated counterfactual surface, the abductive surface wired

below Bronze that borrows this vocabulary is a paraphrase engine with a bibliography, and
you should say so in the review.
Coda: The Line That Runs Backward
Every book in this corpus ends by standing in the room it built, and this room is small and
load-bearing: an engine room.
The walls were up before we got here. Codd gave the world declarative answers and,
without anyone quite voting on it, fixed their direction forever [1]. Marseille set the questions
free and priced the freedom in unification [10, 7]. The database world kept the scale and lost
the modes, then spent a decade forging the lost half by hand and named it after stagecraft [22].
And a 1992 dissertation, written about logic programs when “lakehouse” was not a word,
showed the whole dilemma to be an artifact of postponement: adjoin one value, solve the
recursion as a pair, and the question’s two currents—demand descending, answers ascending—
become ordinary programs, total and fast [33]. Thirty-four years in a drawer, and the drawer,
it turns out, was labeled wrong.
I know. I labeled it.
What this book added is the destination the currents deserved. Pointed at ordinary tables,
the compiled question is an optimization—a good one; take it. Pointed at an honest store, it
is the thing the deductive tradition reached for at its founding and never held: a knowledge
base whose every direction runs. Consequence forward, with types. Warrant backward, as a
walk. Intervention gated, at compile time, before the fluent number does its damage. And
absence—absence, at last, articulate: not an empty result set but a priced question, handed
to the world, with the world’s answer wired to come back as proof.
I keep thinking about the backward line, the one in the diagrams. For a while everyone
treated it as a disease of the formalism—a particle going the wrong way in time, an em
barrassment to be gauged away. The resolution was not to erase the line. The resolution
was to understand that the line was a particle too, the other half of one object, and the
moment physics accepted that, the pictures got simpler and the calculations started coming
out right [74, 77]. Fifty years of data systems have been erasing their backward line—filing it
under provenance, under governance, under magic, under somebody’s memory of why the
number is what it is. This book’s whole argument is four words long: keep the backward
line. It is not a pathology. It is the question, and the question was always half of the object.
And if you want the entire engine as one sentence, take the oldest one, the one that has
been load-bearing since before there were computers to compile anything: you must not fool
yourself, and you are the easiest person to fool. Everything in this book—the modes, the
currents, the four-valued lookup, the gate, the priced frontier—is that sentence, built. A
machine for an institution that would rather know how it knows than sound like it knows.
Nature cannot be fooled [76]; the ledger is how you stop trying.
The equations held. The question compiled. Now go ask it backward.
